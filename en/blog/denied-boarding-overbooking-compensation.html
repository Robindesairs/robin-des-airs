Denied boarding and overbooking: your rights under EC 261

Overbooking is a deliberate airline strategy: they sell more tickets than seats, knowing some passengers won't show up. Most of the time it works. When it doesn't, YOU pay the price — unless you know your rights.

What is denied boarding?

It's when an airline refuses to let you board a flight you have a confirmed reservation for, despite arriving on time and having all required documents. The main causes:

  • Overbooking: too many tickets sold
  • Aircraft change: smaller plane than planned
  • Security/operational reasons (limited cases)

Important: denied boarding is NOT due to your fault (no late arrival, no missing documents, no security issue). If the airline bumps you for one of the reasons above, EC 261 compensation kicks in.

Compensation amounts

Flight distanceCompensation per passenger
≤ 1,500 km€250
1,500-3,500 km (or intra-EU >1,500 km)€400
> 3,500 km outside EU€600

Paid in cash or bank transfer, not in vouchers. The airline cannot force a voucher on you.

Three rights, not one

Denied boarding triggers THREE separate rights:

  1. Compensation (€250-€600 above)
  2. Choice between: full ticket refund within 7 days, OR rerouting at the earliest opportunity, OR rerouting at a later date convenient for you
  3. Right to care: meals, refreshments, hotel if needed, two free phone calls, transport between airport and hotel — for the entire wait

Reduction by 50% in certain cases

The airline can reduce compensation by 50% if it reroutes you AND your arrival delay at final destination is less than:

  • 2 hours for flights ≤ 1,500 km
  • 3 hours for flights 1,500-3,500 km
  • 4 hours for flights > 3,500 km

Beyond these thresholds: you keep the full compensation.

Voluntary vs. involuntary: a critical distinction

Before involuntary denied boarding, the airline must ask for volunteers willing to give up their seat in exchange for "benefits" (voucher, cash, upgrade).

Critical advice: if you volunteer, you LOSE your EC 261 right. The compensation becomes negotiable. Never accept less than the EC 261 amount you would have gotten. Use it as a baseline.

If no volunteer comes forward, the airline picks passengers — typically those with the lowest fare class, last to check in, or no frequent flyer tier. From that moment, you are INVOLUNTARILY denied boarding and the full EC 261 right applies.

What to do at the airport when bumped

  1. Demand a written statement from the airline confirming the denied boarding. Reason "overbooking" or "operational reasons".
  2. Refuse vouchers. Demand cash compensation according to EC 261.
  3. Demand care: meals, hotel, transport if needed. Keep all receipts.
  4. Photograph the departure board showing the original flight time.
  5. Keep the rerouting document (new flight, new boarding pass).

Filing the claim afterwards

The airline often "forgets" to pay the compensation. You must claim it explicitly:

  • Within 1-2 weeks of the flight, write to the airline (email + registered letter)
  • Cite EC 261/2004 article 7 (compensation) and article 4 (denied boarding)
  • Provide IBAN for payment
  • If refused or no reply within 2 months → escalate to NEB or use a specialist service

Frequently Asked Questions

What is denied boarding under EC 261?
It's when an airline refuses to let you board a flight you have a confirmed reservation for. The most common cause is overbooking (the airline sold more tickets than seats). You're entitled to immediate compensation: €250-€600 depending on distance, paid in cash or bank transfer.
How much compensation for denied boarding?
€250 (≤1,500 km), €400 (1,500-3,500 km), €600 (>3,500 km), per passenger. In addition to a full ticket refund or rerouting. Plus right to care (meals, hotel, transport) while waiting.
Can compensation be reduced if I arrive late?
Yes, by 50% if the airline reroutes you and your arrival delay at final destination is limited: less than 2h (short-haul), 3h (medium-haul) or 4h (long-haul). Beyond those thresholds, you keep the full compensation.
Can the airline force me to accept a voucher?
No. Compensation must be paid in CASH (or bank transfer). The airline can OFFER a voucher, but you have the right to refuse and demand cash. Many airlines try to push vouchers — always refuse and demand the cash amount.
What if I volunteered to give up my seat?
If you voluntarily accept the airline's offer (cash, voucher, free upgrade on next flight), you waive your EC 261 compensation right. Negotiate based on the EC 261 amount you would have gotten as a baseline — never accept less.
